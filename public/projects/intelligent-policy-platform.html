01 — The Brief

Where systems complexity meets the need for clarity

The Australian Department of Health and Aged Care manages one of the most interconnected policy landscapes in the world — spanning diverse cohorts, competing priorities, and layered tiers of care. Data lived in silos. No shared map existed. Our brief was to build one — and to make AI a trusted part of how policymakers navigate it.

A system too large to hold in one view
Health policy decisions touch millions of lives across children, First Nations communities, aged populations, and people with disability. Each cohort and domain existed in its own data silo. The opportunity was to design the connective tissue — a shared model that made the whole system legible without flattening its complexity.
Earning trust for AI in a high-consequence domain
Generative AI was entering government at the moment its credibility was still being established. Senior executives needed genuine insight — but equally needed confidence in where that insight came from. This shaped a defining challenge: building an AI experience that was transparent by default, not just capable.
Co-creating the brief, not just answering it
This project started at the concept pitch stage — before a product blueprint, before a stable ontology, before an agreed IA. I helped frame the problem space, shape the system model, and define the UX strategy alongside the team, collaborating across three entities with genuinely different perspectives.
Design question
"How might we help senior policymakers explore the complex, evolving pressures, domains, and enablers of the Australian health system — with genuine confidence in the data, clarity around the AI, and control over the depth of their inquiry?"
Aligning three entities around a shared vision of scope, language, and product intent — across Gravity, Scyne, and DHDA.
Designing Infospec and AI to coexist in the same interface without confusing users about what was verified versus generated.
Letting the ontology shape the UX — domains and tiers were fixed before design began, making the system's structure the product's logic.
Delivering with speed and coherence — concept validation, architecture, and front-end running simultaneously under a compressed timeline.

03 — Process

A research-led journey from ambiguity to delivery

Each phase built on the last — progressively narrowing ambiguity, deepening understanding of users and system, and translating research and structure into designed experience. Insights from later phases regularly fed back into earlier ones.

Phase 01
Problem Framing & Concept Pitch
Stakeholder alignmentValue propositionAI trust modelConcept prototype
I began by framing the product's core purpose — what it should feel like to use, what trust would need to look like in a government AI context, and what the simplest credible version could be. An early concept prototype gave us something tangible to align three entities around. This phase was about creating shared language before shared solutions.
Phase 02
User Research & Requirements
Stakeholder workshopsUser story mappingPRD authoringFeature prioritisation
I led structured workshops across DHDA's policy research teams, senior analysts, and executive stakeholders — approaching each session as research as much as requirements-gathering. Listening carefully to how people described the system, where they felt lost, and what decisions they actually needed to make helped me surface needs that weren't always explicitly stated.
Users rarely say what they need. They describe frustrations, workarounds, and goals. The research job was to hear beneath the surface.
Phase 03
System Architecture & Ontology Design
4-system modelSTEEPLEO frameworkDomain tieringCohort mappingKnowledge graph
Working closely with the ontology architects, I helped translate the four-system health model into something a designer and user could reason from. Domains and tiers were defined through this process — and rather than treating the fixed structure as a constraint on UX, we used it as the product's own logic. The STEEPLEO pressure framework became the analytical spine for AI-generated trend content.
The system's architecture became the interaction model. Pressure → Threat or Opportunity → Domain Impact wasn't just a taxonomy — it became a navigable flow that users could feel, not just understand.
Phase 04
UX Design & Interaction Architecture
Interaction flowsAI trust layerLife course toggleCard architectureScenario simulation UX
With the system architecture as foundation, I designed a two-click exploratory flow: System of Systems → Cohort → Domain → Pressure. The dual-badge trust layer (Verified Infospec / Generated AI) was designed as a visual grammar users could internalise quickly. I also designed the scenario simulation framework — giving the Known Knowns / Unknown Unknowns model a navigable, usable form for policy analysts.
Phase 05
Prototype, Build & Delivery
Figma prototypeVibe-coded front endAI guardrailsExecutive demo
The MVP came together as a coherent, navigable prototype demonstrated to senior DHDA executives — with the AI trust layer, cohort switcher, and domain drill-down all operational. In parallel, my team-mate and I built and shipped the platform management and governance module using vibe coding — from concept to integrated front end.
Design intent stayed intact all the way to delivery. No translation loss between design and engineering — because for this module, they were the same conversation.

05 — Key Insights

What designing in complexity taught us

These weren't observations from the outside. They came from sitting at the centre of the system — where the tensions between policy rigour, AI capability, and user need made themselves visible through the work itself.

"The best way to decode complexity is to dive right in and sit at its centre — not analyse it from a distance. Only when you're inside the system do you see how AI outputs, knowledge availability, data accessibility, and user needs actually come together. Distance gives you a map. Proximity gives you understanding."

01
Vision is your main component — never detach
Product vision is like a Figma main component. You keep editing it, evolving it, letting it grow — but you never detach, because that's what keeps the character of the design coherent across every sprint. Every decision needs to be tied back. That thread is what makes a complex platform feel like one thing.
02
Designing at the intersection of policy, usability, and trust
Every decision had to work on three levels simultaneously: defensible to policy leads, buildable by a small team, navigable by time-poor executives. The two-click flow and AI trust badges weren't flourishes — they were the answer to "how do we make a complex system feel safe to use?"
03
No question is too many questions
In a domain this layered — spanning policy frameworks, clinical ontologies, data governance, and executive decision-making — curiosity is the method, not a sign of confusion. The people who asked the most tended to build the most coherent things.
04
Users will never tell you what they want
They describe frustrations, workarounds, and goals. Policymakers said they wanted "better reports." What they actually needed was a way to see relationships between pressures they hadn't yet connected. Listen for the need underneath the ask.
05
Ontology-first UX is a genuine design method
Starting from a fixed information architecture — rather than user flows — is unusual, but in government and systems domains it can be exactly right. When the system's own structure becomes the product's logic, UX design becomes the act of revealing that structure, not imposing one.
06
Trust is a design problem, not a disclaimer
The dual-badge model built more executive confidence than any policy document could. Visible provenance — knowing exactly where something came from and how confident the system is in it — matters more than invisible accuracy. Show your work. Always.
06 — Learnings & Outcome

What I'm taking forward

The MVP was demonstrated to senior DHDA executives and laid the foundation for phased platform expansion. These are the things that stay with me into every systems project.

01
Vibe coding changed what I could own
Taking the platform management module from concept to integrated front end without a separate engineering sprint meant design intent survived all the way to delivered product. The gap between design and engineering closed — because for this module, they were the same conversation. That's a capability I want to keep building, not outsourcing.
02
Shared artefacts create shared understanding
The PRD wasn't just a document — it was the team's common ground across three entities with genuinely different priorities. In complex, multi-stakeholder projects, a shared anchor is what prevents good intentions from producing divergent outcomes. Invest in the artefact early.
03
Constraint, made visible, builds confidence
Scoping AI to descriptive outputs in MVP and surfacing that constraint explicitly in the interface didn't limit the product — it made it more trustworthy. Users appreciate honesty about what a system can and can't do today. Appropriate humility in a system is a feature, not a concession.
04
Structure that emerges from the system is more durable
When UX flows emerge from the system's own ontological structure rather than being imposed from outside, they're more resilient to scope changes and easier to scale. The information architecture didn't just organise the product — it explained it.
